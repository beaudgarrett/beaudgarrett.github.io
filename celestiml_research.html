Neural Approximation of Celestial Mechanics and Statistical Alignment Rarity Scoring via Feed-Forward Regression

Beau Garrett

Independent Research — beaugarrett.dev

April 2026

Abstract

We present CelestiML, a hybrid computational system that pairs a deep feed-forward neural network with a statistical alignment analysis engine to approximate heliocentric planetary positions and quantify the rarity of multi-body celestial configurations. The regression model accepts 13 cyclically encoded temporal features and predicts three-dimensional Cartesian coordinates for eight planets (24 outputs), trained on ~73,000 ephemeris-derived samples spanning 1900–2100. Using a four-layer architecture with SiLU activations, batch normalization, and Adam optimization with cosine annealing, the model achieves a test RMSE of 0.0648 AU across all bodies, with inner-planet mean errors below 0.04 AU. A complementary alignment detection pipeline classifies pairwise conjunctions, multi-body clustering, and linear formations, producing a composite rarity score benchmarked against a 100-year reference distribution. We demonstrate that lightweight neural regressors can meaningfully approximate Keplerian and perturbative orbital dynamics from temporal features alone, and that heuristic alignment scoring produces stable, interpretable rarity percentiles consistent with known rare astronomical events.

Keywords: neural regression, celestial mechanics, orbital prediction, time-series features, cyclical encoding, planetary alignment, rarity scoring, feed-forward networks

1. Introduction

Predicting planetary positions has historically relied on analytical solutions to Kepler's equations, numerical integration of N-body dynamics, or pre-computed ephemeris tables such as JPL's DE440[1]. While these methods achieve sub-arcsecond precision, they require significant domain-specific infrastructure and do not generalize to the broader question: can a data-driven model learn the latent dynamics of planetary motion from temporal features alone?

Recent advances in physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)[2] and neural ordinary differential equations[3] have demonstrated that neural architectures can approximate physical systems when given appropriate inductive biases. CelestiML explores a deliberately simpler hypothesis: whether a standard feed-forward network, given only cyclically encoded datetime features, can learn to approximate multi-planet heliocentric positions with useful accuracy for interactive visualization.

Beyond position prediction, the system introduces a statistical alignment analysis engine that detects conjunctions, clustering, and linear formations among planetary bodies, assigning each configuration a rarity score benchmarked against a century-scale reference distribution. This combination of neural regression and statistical analysis enables an interactive platform where users can explore any historical or future date and receive both positional data and a quantified assessment of how unusual the planetary arrangement is.

2. Methodology

2.1 Data Generation

Training data was generated using PyEphem[4], a Python interface to the XEphem astronomical computation library. For each sample, heliocentric ecliptic coordinates (longitude, latitude, distance) were computed for all eight major planets and converted to Cartesian (x, y, z) in astronomical units (AU). The dataset spans 1900–2100 at approximately daily resolution with sub-day jitter (uniform 0–12 hour offset) to prevent temporal aliasing, yielding ~73,000 training samples.

The target vector for each sample is a 24-dimensional array: [Mercuryx, Mercuryy, Mercuryz, Venusx, ..., Neptunez]. The data was split 80/10/10 into train, validation, and test sets using a fixed random seed for reproducibility.

2.2 Cyclical Feature Engineering

Planetary motion is inherently periodic, making standard scalar time representations (e.g., Unix timestamps) poorly suited for neural regression. We encode each temporal component as a sin/cos pair to preserve circular continuity, producing 13 input features:

f(t, T) = [sin(2πt / T), cos(2πt / T)]

Applied across five temporal scales: month (T=12), day-of-month (T=31), hour (T=24), day-of-year (T=365.25), decade (T=10yr), and century (T=100yr), plus a linearly normalized year. The multi-scale encoding captures Mercury's 88-day orbit through Neptune's 165-year period within a compact 13-dimensional feature space.

Figure 1. Multi-scale cyclical feature encoding. Short-period oscillations capture inner-planet orbital frequencies (Mercury: 88d, Venus: 225d), while decade and century encodings provide the low-frequency basis needed for outer-planet motion (Uranus: 84yr, Neptune: 165yr).

2.3 Network Architecture

The CelestiMLNet architecture is a five-layer feed-forward regressor implemented in pure NumPy (zero external ML framework dependencies). The design follows the widening-narrowing bottleneck pattern, expanding the 13-dimensional input to 512 hidden units before compressing to the 24-dimensional output space:

Figure 2. CelestiMLNet architecture. A widening-narrowing feed-forward topology maps 13 cyclical temporal features to 24 Cartesian planetary coordinates. Each hidden layer applies batch normalization followed by SiLU (Swish) activation. The output layer is linear (no activation).

The SiLU (Sigmoid Linear Unit) activation σ(x) = x · sigmoid(x) was chosen over ReLU for its smooth gradient landscape, which aids convergence on regression tasks with continuous periodic targets[5]. Batch normalization stabilizes training by normalizing pre-activations to zero mean and unit variance, with learned affine parameters. He initialization[6] scales initial weights by √(2/fan_in) to preserve variance across layers.

2.4 Training Procedure

The model was trained using the Adam optimizer[7] (β1=0.9, β2=0.999, ε=10-8) with cosine annealing learning rate scheduling over a maximum of 300 epochs. The learning rate follows:

lr(t) = lr0 · 0.5 · (1 + cos(π · t / T))

where lr0 = 10-3 and T = 300 epochs. L2 weight decay (10-4) and gradient clipping ([-1, 1]) were applied for regularization. Early stopping with patience of 30 epochs on validation MSE prevented overfitting. The loss function is mean squared error (MSE) over the 24-dimensional output vector. Training was performed in mini-batches of 512 samples.

2.5 Alignment Detection Pipeline

The alignment engine operates on heliocentric ecliptic longitudes and implements three detection algorithms: (a) Pairwise Conjunctions — pairs within a 15° angular threshold; (b) Multi-body Clustering — groups of 3+ planets within a 90° arc, identified via circular spread minimization; (c) Linear Formations — groups of 3+ planets deviating less than 20° from a Sun-centered line. Detected alignments are deduplicated by removing strict subsets of larger configurations.

2.6 Rarity Scoring

A composite rarity score R ∈ [0, 100] is computed from four weighted heuristic components: maximum cluster size within a 90° arc (0–40 points), total angular spread (0–25 points), minimum pairwise angular distance (0–20 points), and conjunction count (0–15 points). The score is calibrated against a reference distribution computed by sampling every 7 days across a 100-year window (1924–2024), yielding ~5,200 reference configurations for percentile computation.

3. Experimental Results

3.1 Training Convergence

The model converged at epoch 187 (early stopping triggered at patience=30 from best validation loss at epoch 157). Training MSE decreased from 4.82 to 0.0038 over the first 100 epochs, with validation loss tracking closely, indicating minimal overfitting. The cosine annealing schedule provided beneficial learning rate warmdown in the final training phase.

Figure 3. Training and validation MSE loss over 187 epochs. The model achieves rapid convergence in the first 80 epochs with cosine-annealed learning rate, settling to a best validation MSE of 0.0041. The narrow train-val gap indicates good generalization without overfitting.

3.3 Inner vs. Outer Planet Analysis

The relative error analysis (Table 1, rightmost column) reveals an inverse relationship between orbital radius and relative prediction difficulty. Mercury's 9.18% relative error reflects the challenge of capturing its rapid 88-day period and high eccentricity from cyclical features. By contrast, Neptune's 0.54% relative error demonstrates that the model accurately captures its near-circular, slow-moving orbit. This suggests that the model effectively learns long-period orbital dynamics through the decade and century encodings, while short-period inner-planet motion remains the primary source of error.

3.4 Alignment Rarity Distribution

Figure 5 shows the distribution of composite rarity scores computed across the 1924–2024 reference window. The distribution is right-skewed, with the majority of dates scoring below 25 ("Common"), confirming that genuinely rare planetary configurations are infrequent. Only 3.2% of sampled dates scored above 50 ("Rare"), and 0.4% above 75 ("Extremely Rare").

Figure 5. Distribution of composite rarity scores across 5,218 sampled dates (1924–2024). The right-skewed distribution confirms that high-scoring configurations are genuinely infrequent, with 96.8% of dates classified as "Common" or "Uncommon."

4. Discussion

The results demonstrate that a modestly sized feed-forward network (~660K parameters) trained on cyclically encoded temporal features can approximate multi-planet heliocentric positions with sufficient accuracy for interactive visualization. The 0.065 AU overall RMSE corresponds to roughly 9.7 million kilometers—significant in absolute terms but well within the tolerance required for visual representations where planetary bodies are rendered with perceptible radii.

The inverse relationship between orbital radius and relative error is notable. The model effectively learns the slow, near-circular dynamics of outer planets through the decade and century feature encodings, while inner-planet accuracy is limited by the feed-forward architecture's inability to maintain temporal state. Recurrent architectures (LSTM, Transformer) could potentially improve inner-planet predictions by explicitly modeling orbital phase continuity, though at the cost of additional complexity and the loss of pure-NumPy portability.

The alignment rarity scoring system produces distributions consistent with astronomical expectations. The May 5, 2000 alignment (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn clustered within ~25°) receives a score of 72/100, correctly classified as "Rare." The system's heuristic scoring avoids the computational expense of full Monte Carlo simulation while maintaining interpretability through its component-based structure.

A key design decision was the implementation in pure NumPy without PyTorch, TensorFlow, or JAX. This eliminates all ML framework dependencies from the production backend, reduces the deployment footprint, and serves as a pedagogical demonstration that backpropagation, batch normalization, and Adam optimization can be implemented from first principles. The tradeoff is training speed (~3x slower than equivalent PyTorch) and the absence of GPU acceleration.

5. Conclusion

CelestiML demonstrates that neural regression over cyclically encoded temporal features provides a viable approximation of multi-planet heliocentric dynamics for interactive applications. The feed-forward architecture achieves sub-0.04 AU mean error for inner planets and sub-0.17 AU for outer planets, with relative errors below 1.5% for all bodies except Mercury. The accompanying alignment detection and rarity scoring pipeline produces stable, interpretable assessments calibrated against a century-scale reference distribution.

Future work includes: (a) temporal architectures (Transformer encoder with positional embeddings derived from orbital periods) to improve phase-sensitive inner-planet prediction; (b) incorporating gravitational perturbation terms as auxiliary features to model Jupiter-Saturn resonance effects; (c) extending the alignment system to geocentric apparent positions for observer-centric analysis; and (d) uncertainty quantification via Monte Carlo dropout or ensemble methods.
